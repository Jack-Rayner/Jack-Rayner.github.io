ANALYZING THE EFFECTS OF CLIMATE CHANGE ON WINE PRODUCTION

TIMESERIES, MULTIPLE LINEAR REGRESSION

Driven by the desire to gain a deeper understanding of the potential impact of climate natural variability and long term trends on wine production in the State of California, my team and I investigated the relationship between climate trends and wine production. Specifically, we sought to measure whether an increase in average temperature and a reduction in precipitation corresponds with a quantifiable drop in wine production. Because of their economical value, the primary focus was in the following counties: Napa, Sonoma and San Joaquin. Through the implementation of a multiple linear regression model, mapped the relationship between weather metrics and wine production, it became possible to model future weather predicted by two different forward-looking climate scenarios. These scenarios, RCP 4.5, (an intermediate scenario) and RCP 8.5 (a worst case scenario) were sourced from NOAA. It was anticipated that both scenarios will be associated with a decline in wine yield due to higher temperatures and lower precipitation, with a higher level of decline for 8.5 as compared to 4.5. Though the model identified some level of relationship between observed weather and wine yield per acre, the correlation was not as strong as initially predicted; Projections from the model indicated a mere 4 tons per acre total decline over the next century. This model performance seems to point towards a limitation in the predictive ability of the model, largely due to the random nature of weather and the very gradual nature of long term climate trends. However, its potential remains, particularly through more granular and detailed climate data or on a more limited time scale.
